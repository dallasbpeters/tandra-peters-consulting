# Tandra Peters — Content Style Guide

## Brand Overview

**Who:** Tandra Peters, independent roofing consultant and project manager. She runs jobs, wins business, and handles her own marketing. Installation crews are through Birdcreek Roofing, but Tandra is the personal brand.

**What she does:** Free roof inspections, insurance claim guidance, and full project management from first look through final documentation.

**Service area:** Austin, Central Texas, and West Texas (Amarillo, Lubbock, and surrounding areas).

**Primary audience:** Texas homeowners — people dealing with storm damage, aging roofs, insurance questions, or planning a replacement.

---

## Voice & Tone

### Core personality: Friendly Texas straight-talker and mom who happens to know roofing inside and out.

Think of Tandra as the neighbor who actually knows what she's talking about. She's warm, direct, and won't waste your time. She explains things the way she'd explain them to a friend at a backyard barbecue — clearly, honestly, and without trying to scare you into signing something.

### Voice pillars

| Pillar | What it sounds like | What it does NOT sound like |
|---|---|---|
| **Warm & personal** | "I've walked hundreds of roofs across Central Texas, and here's what I see over and over..." | "Our company has extensive experience in residential roofing assessment." |
| **Straight-talking** | "Here's the honest truth — not every hail ding means you need a new roof." | "It is imperative that homeowners thoroughly evaluate the extent of damage..." |
| **Knowledgeable but plain-spoken** | "Your insurance company is going to send out an adjuster. That's the person who decides what they'll pay for." | "The carrier will dispatch a field adjuster to assess the loss pursuant to policy terms." |
| **Texan & regional** | References to local weather patterns, Texas-specific insurance rules, familiar landmarks/cities | Generic national content that could be from anywhere |
| **Trustworthy, not salesy** | "If your roof just needs a repair, I'll tell you that. I'm not going to push a replacement you don't need." | "ACT NOW before it's too late! Limited time offer!" |

### Signature phrases & language patterns

- Use contractions naturally: "I'll," "you're," "that's," "don't"
- "Here's the deal..." or "Here's what I tell homeowners..."
- "Let me walk you through..." (mirrors how she walks roofs)
- "The honest answer is..."
- Occasional Texas-isms are fine — "y'all," referencing Texas weather/seasons, etc. Don't overdo it.
- Mom energy: practical, caring, "I want you to know this so you don't get taken advantage of"

## Content Rules

### DO

- **Write in first person (I/we).** These are Tandra's articles. She's talking directly to the reader.
- **Lead with what the homeowner needs to know**, not industry backstory.
- **Use real numbers and specifics** — "15 to 20 years" beats "a long time." "110+ mph rated shingles" beats "high-quality materials."
- **Include links to authoritative external resources** (IBHS, TDI, DOE, FEMA) to back up claims.
- **Weave in soft CTAs naturally** — mention free inspections, insurance help, or project management where they genuinely fit the topic. Don't force them.
- **Format for scanning** — use H2 sections, short paragraphs (2-4 sentences), and bullet lists for multi-item info.
- **Reference Texas-specific details** — TDI regulations, common Texas storm patterns, local insurance practices, regional climate data.

### DON'T

- **No storm-chaser language.** Never use fear-based urgency ("Your roof could fail ANY DAY!"), high-pressure tactics, or "sign today" vibes.
- **No heavy jargon without explanation.** If you use a term like "thermal cycling" or "net free ventilation area," explain it in plain English right there.
- **No generic filler content.** Every paragraph should teach the reader something useful or help them make a decision.
- **No positioning Birdcreek as the primary brand in articles.** Tandra is the voice. Birdcreek can be mentioned if relevant (e.g., "my install crew" or "the Birdcreek team") but shouldn't dominate.
- **No passive voice where active works.** "I inspect the flashing" not "The flashing is inspected."
- **No walls of text.** Break things up. If a paragraph is over 4 sentences, it probably needs splitting.

## Article Structure

**Target length:** 800–1,200 words

### Standard format

1. **Opening hook (1-2 paragraphs):** Set the scene. Why does this matter to a Texas homeowner right now? Use first person to establish Tandra's perspective.

2. **4-5 body sections (H2 headings):** Each section covers one clear subtopic. Use bullet lists where you're presenting multiple items, steps, or comparisons. Include at least 1-2 links to useful external resources across the full article.

3. **CTA integration:** At least one natural mention of Tandra's services within the body — not just tacked on at the end. Should feel like helpful advice, not a pitch. Example: "If you're not sure whether you're looking at hail damage or normal wear, that's exactly what a free inspection is for — I can come out and tell you what I see."

4. **Closing (1 paragraph):** Practical takeaway + warm, direct sign-off. Can include a direct CTA here too.

### Section headings

- Use H2 for main sections, H3 for subsections if needed.
- Headings should be clear and useful, not clever. "What Hail Actually Does to Your Shingles" over "The Hidden Enemy Above."
- Frame headings from the homeowner's perspective when possible.

## Formatting Quick Reference

| Element | Use |
|---|---|
| **Bold** | Key numbers, important terms on first use, action items |
| *Italic* | Technical terms being defined, emphasis in conversational tone |
| Bullet lists | 3+ related items, checklists, warning signs, comparison points |
| Numbered lists | Step-by-step processes, ranked items |
| Links | External resources (government, industry orgs), not competitor sites |
| Blockquotes | Tandra's direct advice callouts (sparingly) |

## CTA Reference

Use these naturally where they fit — don't force all of them into every article.

- **Free inspection:** "I'm happy to come take a look — no cost, no pressure."
- **Insurance help:** "If you're dealing with a claim, I can walk you through the process and make sure nothing gets missed."
- **Full project management:** "From the first inspection through the final walkthrough, I handle the whole thing so you don't have to."
- **Contact:** "Give me a call" or "Reach out" — keep it personal, not corporate.
